# Holograms

In our universe, one of the more surprising results in theoretical physics is the holographic principle, which suggests that the information content of a volume of space can be described by the information on its boundary.  For black holes specifically, the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy is proportional to the surface area of the event horizon, not its volume.  This result has generated decades of debate and speculation.

In Universe X, the holographic nature of black holes is obvious.

Consider a room that is completely sealed off from the outside world, except for a single light bulb connected to a computer outside the room.  Every hour the bulb either turns on, turns off or stays the same.  It does not matter how complicated the computer is.  It does not matter how sophisticated its algorithms are or how much memory it has.  There is still only 1 bit of information per hour available to anyone inside the room.

The complexity of a system as perceived from the outside is entirely determined by the size of its interface, not by the complexity of its interior.

For a black hole in Universe X, the event horizon is the surface where the aexels flow inward at the speed of light.  No signal from the interior can cross this boundary outward.  Regardless of what is happening inside, regardless of how much matter is contained within, regardless of how complex the arrangements of teslons and edisons might be, the only information the outside universe can ever interact with is what sits on that surface.

The number of aexels situated on the event horizon is the total channel capacity between the interior and the exterior.  That is the entirety of the interface.  The information content of a black hole is bounded by its surface because its surface is the only thing the rest of the universe can talk to.

For any other object in Universe X, one can always probe deeper.  Signals can be sent in and received back.  Any arbitrary shell around a planet or a star can be looked through.  The surface is not a constraint because it is not an information boundary.

The black hole is the unique case where the surface truly is the entirety of the interface.  And at that point, the holographic principle is not mysterious at all.  It is as obvious as a light bulb in a sealed room.
