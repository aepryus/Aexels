# Speed of Light

In the Cellular Automata article, the speed of light in Universe X was defined as one aexel per tic.  This was a useful and intuitive starting point, but it is not precisely correct.  It is a stepping stone.

Consider that any motion through the aether will not necessarily line up with the geometry of the aexels themselves.  The aexels form a three dimensional lattice and a signal moving through that lattice must jump from aexel to aexel.  If the desired direction of travel does not align with the lattice, each jump will carry the signal at some angle θ from the intended direction.  The effective speed in any given direction is therefore reduced by a factor of cos(θ), where θ is the average misalignment angle.

But there is a deeper issue.  If we were physicists living inside Conway's Game of Life, would we consider the speed of light to be one cell per time step?  We would not.  We would observe the glider, the fastest translating pattern in our world, and note that it moves one cell diagonally every 4 time steps.  We would call that c/4 our speed of light.

The speed of light as observed by the inhabitants of a cellular automaton is not the raw lattice speed; it is the speed of the fastest stable translating pattern that their physics supports.  The relationship between the two involves the internal mechanics of that pattern.

In Universe X, the ping itself is not a bare signal hopping between neighbors.  It is an edison; a stable state loop with structure.  It carries a cupola vector; it has orientation.  Whatever the actual propagation speed turns out to be, it will be determined by the mechanics of how these objects traverse the lattice, not by the idealized one-aexel-per-tic lattice speed.

The speed of light in Universe X is the speed of light.  Calling it one aexel per tic was a useful approximation, but the true value will only be known once the source code of the aexel is understood.
